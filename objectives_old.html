Cyber Safety

Protecting Children Online — Selected EU, National and Regional Laws and Initiatives

Maria Niestadt, April 2025  ·  EPRS | European Parliamentary Research Service
  • Enforce existing EU child-online-safety regulations (DSA, GDPR, AVMSD) fully and consistently across member states
  • Prohibit profiling-based advertising targeting minors and restrict algorithmic amplification of harmful content to children
  • Mandate age-appropriate design and default privacy settings for platforms likely accessed by children
  • Strengthen obligations for platforms to detect, remove and report child sexual abuse material (CSAM) promptly
  • Establish specialised multi-agency response units combining law enforcement, social services and digital platform cooperation
  • Classify compulsive digital use as a public health risk and create specialised treatment pathways
  • Fund large-scale, evidence-based digital literacy and online safety education programmes in schools
  • Guarantee children the right to erasure of their digital footprint and prohibit sharing of children's biometric data without explicit parental consent
Creativity & Critical Thinking

Fostering Students' Creativity and Critical Thinking: What It Means in School

OECD, 2019
  • Make creativity and critical thinking explicit learning objectives across all subjects at both primary and secondary levels
  • Integrate creativity and critical thinking into regular subject teaching rather than treating them as standalone or generic skills
  • Adopt teacher-friendly OECD rubrics to clarify, teach and assess creativity and critical thinking in classrooms
  • Use creativity and critical-thinking rubrics formatively — for lesson design, student feedback and self-assessment — not only for grading
  • Redesign or adapt lesson plans using structured design criteria that embed creativity and critical thinking while maintaining curriculum content
  • Develop students' creativity and critical thinking through open, challenging, multi-perspective tasks that allow for risk-taking and reflection
  • Provide continuous, practice-embedded teacher professional development combining training, individual support and peer learning
  • Support professional learning communities so teachers can collaboratively adapt tools and share effective practices
  • Assess creativity and critical thinking separately from technical subject mastery, using clear progression levels
  • Recognise both learning processes and final products when assessing creativity and critical thinking
  • Validate interventions rigorously before scaling them up across systems or countries
  • Align curriculum expectations, teaching practices and assessment approaches to support creativity and critical thinking consistently
  • Treat creativity and critical thinking as core future-oriented skills essential for innovation, wellbeing and democratic participation
Media Literacy

Fostering a Key Civic Skill in a Digital Information Environment

Tarja Laaninen, May 2025  ·  EPRS | European Parliamentary Research Service
  • Develop dynamic media literacy curricula that evolve alongside the changing digital information environment
  • Support educators to bridge school and out-of-school media practices
  • Raise awareness about disinformation and misuse of data in education
  • Invest in research on sense-making and inoculation approaches to building resilience against false information
  • Expand research on media literacy across all school levels
  • Facilitate large-scale collaboration initiatives between educational institutions, civil society and media organisations
  • Align media and digital literacy policies with teacher education and continuous professional development
  • Support inclusion of media literacy in OECD PISA assessments
AI Literacy

A Rapid Review of AI Literacy Frameworks, with Policy Recommendations

Dr Veli Hillman, Prof. Wayne Holmes, Tania Duarte (We and AI)  ·  The Royal Society
A. Address the Needs of the UK
  • Be clear who AI literacy is for
  • Define AI literacy clearly and comprehensively
  • Prioritise the human dimension of AI
  • Create a coherent UK-wide approach
B. Build the Infrastructure for Educational Success
  • Create a national multi-stakeholder AI literacy task force
  • Prioritise teacher training and development
  • Resource AI literacy with clear, realistic funding models
  • Guard against undue corporate influence
C. Take an Iterative and Responsive Approach
  • Commission pilot programmes and longitudinal research
  • Align with existing digital, media and data literacy frameworks
  • Develop AI-literacy-specific assessment tools
  • Map and support grassroots AI literacy initiatives
D. Create a Future-Ready UK
  • Embed AI literacy across the curriculum
  • Address equity and accessibility
  • Safeguard children
  • Connect classrooms to society
